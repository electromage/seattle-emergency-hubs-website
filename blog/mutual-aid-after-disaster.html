Mutual Aid

How Mutual Aid Works After a Disaster

December 10, 2025 · Outreach Team

Mutual aid is a simple concept: neighbors helping neighbors. It's the idea that communities are stronger when people support one another, not just as charity, but as a collective response to shared need. After a disaster, mutual aid can literally save lives.

What Mutual Aid Looks Like in Practice

After a major earthquake, here's what mutual aid looks like in a neighborhood with a functioning hub:

  • A volunteer team does welfare checks on elderly and disabled neighbors who may be trapped or unable to call for help
  • Neighbors with generators share power for refrigerating medications
  • Hub supply caches are opened to distribute water, food, and first-aid supplies
  • Parents without childcare share the responsibility so other volunteers can work
  • Bilingual neighbors serve as translators for those who don't speak English
  • People with trucks help move debris or transport injured neighbors

Why Hubs Are the Key

Mutual aid happens organically in any community. But without coordination, it's chaotic and inefficient. Emergency hubs provide the structure:

  • A known location where people know to gather
  • Trained volunteers who can assess needs and coordinate tasks
  • Pre-positioned supplies that can be quickly distributed
  • Communication links with the broader emergency response

How to Get Ready Now

The relationships that enable mutual aid are built before disasters, not during them. The best thing you can do is:

  1. Meet your neighbors now — know who lives on your block
  2. Find or start a hub in your neighborhood
  3. Take a CERT or first-aid course
  4. Share your skills with the hub (whether that's first aid, translation, or driving a truck)

Find your local hub to get started.
