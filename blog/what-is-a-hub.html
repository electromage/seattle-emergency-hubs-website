What Is an Emergency Hub?

February 1, 2026 · Outreach Team

An emergency hub is a pre-designated neighborhood gathering point where residents can come together after a disaster. Staffed by trained volunteers, hubs serve as a coordination center for mutual aid, information sharing, and basic emergency services while the formal response ramps up.

The Problem Hubs Solve

After a major disaster — like a large earthquake — 911 services are quickly overwhelmed, roads may be impassable, and it can take days for help to reach every neighborhood. Historically, communities that recover fastest are those with strong social ties and pre-existing neighborhood networks.

Emergency hubs are designed to strengthen those networks before a disaster happens, so they're ready to activate immediately when one does.

What Happens at a Hub After a Disaster?

  • Neighbors check in and report injuries or damage in the area
  • Trained volunteers provide basic first aid
  • The hub becomes an information point for neighbors without power or communications
  • Supply caches are opened to distribute emergency supplies
  • Volunteers help with light search and rescue and resident welfare checks
  • Hubs relay information to Seattle OEM and coordinate with the broader response

How Is a Hub Started?

It takes just a few motivated neighbors to start a hub. The basic steps are:

  1. Identify a meeting location (a park, school, community center, or even a neighbor's yard)
  2. Recruit a small team of volunteers — ideally 5 to 10 to start
  3. Connect with Seattle Emergency Hubs for training, resources, and support
  4. Hold regular meetings to build relationships and skills

We provide training, supplies, and ongoing coordination support to every hub in our network. Contact us to get started.
